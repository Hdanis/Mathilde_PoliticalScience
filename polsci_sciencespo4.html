The Science of Politics

Cyril Benoit
01 Executive TL;DR
📝 Exam tip — this tab = your Summary section (20%). Read it, then write it in your own words.
⚡ 60-second pitch

Political science is the systematic, evidence-based study of how collective choices are made and who prevails in making them. Unlike philosophy or history, it aims to produce generalizable knowledge — not just describe events, but explain why they happen. Its methods are deliberately diverse (formal, qualitative, quantitative) because politics itself is diverse. The course's core claim: to study politics rigorously, you need shared tools — concepts, theories, and variables — and a clear distinction between what is (positive) and what ought to be (normative).

1.1 — What is Politics?

The course opens with Lasswell's famous definition: politics is about "who gets what, when and how." This is deliberately broad. Politics is not just elections or governments — it is any process by which collective choices are made that have a lasting impact on the life of a community. This includes the state, institutions, laws, international agreements, redistributive policies, and the conflicts these generate.

What makes political science a science is not its object (other disciplines also study politics) but its method: the deliberate pursuit of generalizable, evidence-based knowledge. As Almond (1996) puts it, political science aims to produce "inferences or generalizations about politics drawn from evidence." This distinguishes it from political philosophy (normative) or political commentary (descriptive without theory).

1.2 — The Diversity of Methods

Political science borrows from every social science. Its methods fall into three families. Formal and mathematical methods (decision theory, game theory) model actors making rational choices under constraints — useful for understanding strategic behavior. Qualitative methods (ethnography, interviews, archival research, process-tracing) go deep into specific cases to understand mechanisms. Quantitative methods (econometrics, experiments, text-as-data, network analysis) test hypotheses across large numbers of cases.

This diversity is a strength, not a weakness — but it requires a common language. Different sub-disciplines (comparative politics, international relations, public policy, political behavior, political theory) are more or less connected to each other, but all share the same basic logic of theory-building and empirical testing.

1.3 — The Basic Toolkit: Concepts, Theories, Variables

Concepts are the building blocks: invented categories that help us identify and distinguish phenomena (power, democracy, ideology). They are not natural objects — they are tools, and how you define them shapes what you see. Theories are sets of connected statements that derive propositions from assumptions and test them empirically. A theory is not a guess — it is a structured argument with testable implications. Variables are the measurable operationalizations of concepts: Y is what we want to explain (the outcome), X is what we think causes it.

This logic applies to both quantitative and qualitative research. Whether you are running a regression or doing an interview, you are still asking: what is my concept? what is my theory? what varies and why?

1.4 — Positive vs. Normative

A crucial distinction runs through the entire course. Positive political science describes and explains the world as it is — it makes empirically testable claims. Normative political science evaluates the world and prescribes how it ought to be — it makes value judgments. The course takes an explicitly positive approach: the goal is to understand political phenomena, not to advocate for particular outcomes.

This does not mean political scientists have no values — but it means they must be transparent about when they are making empirical claims versus normative ones. Confusing the two is one of the most common errors in political analysis.

04 Theoretical Debates
Positive vs. Normative Political Science
Positive
Describes and explains the world as it is. Makes empirically testable claims. Example: "Higher income is correlated with higher voter turnout." Can be confirmed or refuted with data.
Normative
Evaluates and prescribes how the world ought to be. Makes value judgments. Example: "Compulsory voting would make democracy more legitimate." Cannot be falsified — depends on prior values.
What this reveals → The distinction is not about which is more important, but about intellectual honesty. Confusing the two is a common error: presenting a value judgment as if it were an empirical finding. The course takes a positive approach — but recognizes normative questions motivate much of the research.
Quantitative vs. Qualitative vs. Formal Methods
Quantitative
Tests hypotheses across many cases using statistics. Strong on generalizability. Weak on mechanisms (why does X cause Y?).
Qualitative / Formal
Goes deep into specific cases (or builds deductive models). Strong on mechanisms and internal validity. Weak on generalizability.
What this reveals → The course does not privilege one method. The appropriate method depends on the research question. Good political science uses the method best suited to the claim — and is transparent about the trade-offs.

Worked example — prompt "Science of Politics" (Class 1)

Your question: "Can political science be truly scientific? A critical assessment of its methods and ambitions."

Part I — The scientific case: Political science produces generalizable, evidence-based knowledge (Almond 1996). Its toolkit — RCTs, quasi-experiments, formal models — mirrors the natural sciences. Positivism: objective reality, discoverable laws, value-neutrality.

Part II — The constructivist challenge + plot twist: Politics is not a physical object — it is socially constructed. Concepts like "sovereignty" or "democracy" only exist because we collectively agree they do. Constructivism shows the positivist ideal of value-neutrality is itself a political choice. The researcher is part of the social world being studied.

Conclusion + beyond class: Political science is neither purely scientific nor purely interpretive. Its strength is methodological pluralism — using the right tool for the right question. The real risk is not that it lacks a unified method, but that it mistakes method for objectivity. ⚖️ Strengths & Weaknesses — Positivism vs. Constructivism
✓ Positivism — Strengths
  • Produces generalizable, falsifiable claims
  • Enables comparison across large numbers of cases
  • Clear criteria for evaluating evidence
  • Strong track record in causal inference (RCTs, quasi-experiments)
✗ Positivism — Weaknesses
  • Value-neutrality is itself a normative claim
  • Struggles with meaning, interpretation, context
  • Measurement can create false precision
  • Cannot tell us what ought to be done
✓ Constructivism — Strengths
  • Captures meaning, context, intersubjectivity
  • Explains how categories (race, sovereignty) become real
  • Sensitive to historical and cultural specificity
  • Researcher reflexivity as strength, not weakness
✗ Constructivism — Weaknesses
  • Findings are hard to generalize
  • Risk of relativism: if all knowledge is constructed, what counts as better evidence?
  • Less equipped for causal claims
  • Can become circular (explaining construction by construction)

Power

01 Executive TL;DR
📝 Exam tip — this tab = your Summary section (20%). Read it, then write it in your own words.
⚡ 60-second pitch

Power has three faces: directive (A forces B to act), gatekeeping (controlling what reaches the agenda), and conceptual (shaping how people think). The deeper you go, the harder power is to see — and the harder to resist. Weber shows domination requires legitimacy to be stable: pure coercion breaks down. Foucault radicalizes this: power is not just repressive but productive — it creates knowledge, shapes identities, and operates through individuals rather than against them. The key insight: the most effective power is the power you don't notice.

2.1 — The Three Faces of Power (Carpenter 2010)

Directive power is the most intuitive: A gets B to do something B would not otherwise do (Dahl 1957). It is visible, observable, behavioral — measured by who wins in actual decisions. This is the framework of pluralism (Dahl) and elite theory (Mills): the question is simply who prevails. Its limit: it only captures power when it results in observable action.

Gatekeeping power is subtler: the ability to control what reaches the agenda. Some issues never become decisions because powerful actors prevent them from being raised. Schattschneider's formula is key: "organization is the mobilization of bias" — every institutional arrangement systematically favors some conflicts and suppresses others. The tobacco industry's management of the scientific agenda (Pinto 2017) is the paradigmatic contemporary case: firms shaped not just what was known, but what was even investigated.

Conceptual power is the deepest: the ability to shape how people think — what categories they use, what seems natural or inevitable. Census categories, regulatory definitions, racial classifications — all reflect and reproduce power by making certain ways of seeing the world appear neutral (Carpenter 2010). Bourdieu calls these "classification struggles." Gramsci calls it hegemony. Foucault calls it knowledge/power.

2.2 — Elitism vs. Pluralism: The American Debate

The directive power framework generated the most influential debate in American political science. C. Wright Mills argued in The Power Elite (1956) that power in America was concentrated in an interlocking set of corporate, military, and political elites occupying "strategic command posts." Robert Dahl (1961) challenged this in his study of New Haven: studying actual decisions across different policy areas, he showed that different groups prevailed in different arenas — no single elite dominated everywhere. Power, Dahl concluded, is distributed pluralistically.

The debate was not just empirical but methodological: how do you study power? If you only look at decisions, you miss the issues that never get decided (gatekeeping). If you look at agenda-setting, you face the problem of proving a negative. Lukes (2005) showed that both approaches miss the deepest face: the power that shapes preferences themselves.

2.3 — Domination and Legitimacy (Weber)

Weber distinguishes power (the capacity to impose your will despite resistance) from domination (when commands are regularly obeyed). Domination is only stable when it is recognized as legitimate. Three ideal-types of legitimate domination: traditional (custom and habit — the monarch rules because this is how it has always been), charismatic (the exceptional personal qualities of the leader), and legal-rational (rule by formal rules and bureaucratic organizations). Modern states are primarily legal-rational: citizens obey laws, not persons.

2.4 — Foucault: Power as Productive

Foucault breaks with the idea that power is simply a repressive force held by some actors over others. For Foucault, power is productive — it creates knowledge, shapes subjectivities, produces the very categories through which we understand ourselves. Power operates "through" individuals rather than "against" them: the prisoner who internalizes surveillance, the student who disciplines herself, the worker who manages her own performance. The prison, the school, the hospital, the military — all are sites of what Foucault calls disciplinary power.

The implication for political analysis: looking only at who gives orders and who obeys misses the pervasive, decentralized ways power shapes what is thinkable, sayable, and doable. "Its success is proportional to its ability to hide its own mechanisms."

04 Theoretical Debates
Elitism vs. Pluralism — Who Holds Power in America?
Mills (Elitism) · 1956
Power is concentrated in an interlocking corporate-military-political elite. They "occupy the strategic command posts" of society. Ordinary citizens have no meaningful influence. The appearance of democratic choice masks real concentration.
Dahl (Pluralism) · 1961
Study of New Haven shows different actors prevail on different issues. No single elite dominates all policy areas. Power is dispersed across competing interest groups. The correct question is not "who rules?" but "who rules in what domain?"
What this reveals → The outcome depends on method. Dahl's pluralism requires observable decisions — but Bachrach & Baratz show this misses agenda control. The debate is still live: contemporary work on lobbying, media ownership, and campaign finance suggests power is more concentrated than Dahl thought, but less monolithic than Mills claimed.
Weber vs. Foucault — What is Domination?
Weber · 1978
Domination = probability of obedience. It requires legitimacy to be stable. Three types: traditional, charismatic, legal-rational. Power is held by identifiable actors in identifiable institutions. The state has a monopoly on legitimate violence.
Foucault · 1975–1980
Power is not held — it is exercised throughout social relations. It is productive, not just repressive. Domination is the extreme case where power relations freeze. The question is not "who has power?" but "how does power circulate, what does it produce?"
What this reveals → Weber explains formal political authority (why the state is obeyed). Foucault explains how power operates outside and beneath formal authority — through norms, disciplines, categories. Both are needed: one to analyze institutions, the other to analyze the ideological conditions that make those institutions seem natural.

Your question: "Is power always visible? A critical assessment of its three faces."

Introduction: Hook — Dahl (1961) and Mills (1956) studied the same country and reached opposite conclusions. Why? Problematize — because they were looking for different things. Foucault (1980): power is productive, not just repressive — it operates "through" individuals. The more effective power becomes, the less it is seen.

Conclusion + beyond class: The Dahl-Mills debate was not a disagreement about facts but about which face of power to look for. Contemporary relevance: algorithmic curation (Meta, TikTok) exercises conceptual power at scale — shaping what millions of people believe is "normal" political opinion without any visible coercion.

01 Executive TL;DR

⚡ 60-second pitch

Do ideas matter, or are they just rationalizations of material interests? This class argues: both matter, in dynamic interaction. Ideology is a coherent, prescriptive and empirical set of ideas about politics — four main types have structured Western democracies: conservatism, liberalism, Christian democracy, socialism. The "end of ideology" thesis (Bell, Fukuyama) has been repeatedly proven wrong — ideological conflict persists, evolves, and returns. Hall's concept of policy paradigm shows how ideas structure what policymakers see as possible, explaining why major policy changes (Keynesianism → monetarism) involve not just new tools but new ways of seeing the problem.

3.1 — Do Ideas Matter?

The class opens with a real tension. For Marxists, ideas are superstructure — they reflect material interests and class positions, serving as ideological cover for the dominant class (Althusser 1970). For rational choice theorists, ideas are at best "focal points" — shared reference points that help self-interested actors coordinate, but with no independent causal role. Against both, a growing body of research (Campbell 2002, Hay 2002) argues that ideas independently shape political outcomes: "Ideas provide the point of mediation between actors and their environment" (Hay 2002). You cannot derive policy preferences from material interests alone — actors need interpretive frameworks to understand what their interests even are.

3.2 — Defining Ideology

Ideology, according to Newton & van Deth (2010), is "a more or less systematic, well-developed and comprehensive set of ideas about politics consisting of both (empirical) statements about what is, and (prescriptive) statements about what ought to be." Four features qualify a set of ideas as an ideology: complexity and abstraction (a systematic worldview, not just opinions), empirical explanation (a claim to explain the world), normative prescription (a vision of what good government looks like), and a plan for action (beliefs about how political goals should be achieved).

The four main ideologies that structured Western political competition in the 20th century: conservatism (preserve existing institutions, pessimistic view of human nature, inequality as natural), liberalism (limited state, individual rights, free market, separation of powers), Christian democracy (natural law, family and community over class, subsidiarity), socialism/social democracy (equality, mixed economy, redistribution).

3.3 — The End of Ideology and Its Returns

From the 1960s onward, Bell (1960) and Lipset (1960) argued that Western democracies had reached a post-ideological consensus around liberalism, democracy, and the welfare state. Fukuyama (1992) extended this after the Cold War: Western liberal democracy had definitively triumphed. The argument: technical expertise, not grand ideological battles, would solve modern governance problems.

This thesis has been repeatedly challenged by reality: the persistence of traditional ideologies, the emergence of new ones (environmentalism, populism), the return of nationalist and socialist alternatives after the 2008 financial crisis. The rise of populism is particularly interesting: is it a symptom of the end of ideology (rejection of all established frameworks) or a new ideological matrix in the making?

3.4 — Hall's Policy Paradigm: Ideas in Action

Peter Hall (1993) offers the most influential account of how ideas shape policy outcomes. A policy paradigm is the broad interpretive framework within which policymakers work — it defines what problems exist, what solutions are legitimate, and what is even thinkable. The shift from Keynesianism to monetarism in the 1970s–1980s is the paradigm case: it was not just a change in policy instruments, but a "third-order change" — a transformation in the very ideas that structured what counted as an economic problem and what counted as a solution. Monetarism did not win because it was empirically better; it won because stagflation discredited Keynesian predictions, opening a window for a new interpretive framework.

04 Theoretical Debates
Materialism vs. Idealism — Do Ideas Have Independent Causal Power?
Materialism (Marx / Althusser)
Ideas are superstructure — they reflect and legitimize material interests. The real drivers of politics are class, property, and economic structures. Ideas are "a mere rhetorical distraction" or ideological cover. Althusser (1970): the ideological state apparatus maintains capitalist domination.
Idealism (Weber / Hall / Hay)
Ideas independently shape outcomes. Weber: Protestant ethics drove capitalism — not the reverse. Hall (1993): paradigm shifts change what policymakers can even conceive of doing. Hay (2002): ideas mediate between actors and environment — you cannot derive preferences from interests alone.
What this reveals → The current consensus (Hay 2002) is a synthesis: ideas and material factors are in dynamic interaction. Neither fully determines the other. This is the most defensible position in a dissertation — pure materialism cannot explain why identical material conditions produce different policies; pure idealism cannot explain why some ideas win and others lose.
End of Ideology vs. Return of Ideology
End of ideology thesis (Bell 1960, Fukuyama 1992)
Post-WWII consensus on liberal democracy, welfare state, mixed economy. Cold War's end = ideological battle settled. Future politics = technical problem-solving, not ideological conflict. "Grand passions over ideas were exhausted."
Return of ideology (empirical evidence)
Rise of Green politics, religious fundamentalism, populism, nationalism. 2008 crisis reignited socialist alternatives. Increasing ideological polarization in the US (Lupton et al. 2017). Vaccine hesitancy correlates with ideology (Baumgaertner 2018). Ideology is back — if it ever left.
What this reveals → The "end of ideology" thesis confused the temporary exhaustion of mid-century ideological passions with a permanent structural change. Ideology did not end — it mutated. New cleavages (cosmopolitan vs. nationalist, green vs. growth) replaced old ones (capital vs. labor), but the ideological structure of politics remains.

Institutions

Cyril Benoit
01 Executive TL;DR
📝 Exam tip — this tab = your Summary section (20%). Read it, then write it in your own words.
⚡ 60-second pitch

Institutions are the rules of the game — formal and informal — that simultaneously constrain and enable human action. The same interests and cleavages can produce radically different political outcomes depending on the institutional context (electoral systems, committee rules, constitutional structures). Three schools disagree on why institutions exist and how they change: rational choice (they solve collective action problems), historical (they are inherited from the past and change through critical junctures and path dependency), sociological (they embody cultural norms and frames). All three agree: institutions shape outcomes independently of individual preferences.

4.1 — What are Institutions?

North's (1990) definition is the most cited: institutions are "the humanly devised constraints that structure political, economic and social interaction" — comprising both informal constraints (customs, norms, taboos) and formal rules (constitutions, laws, property rights). The key word is "humanly devised": institutions are not natural, they are built and can be changed. Moe (2015) extends this: political institutions are not just formal government structures but the entire set of arrangements surrounding and maintaining them. Hodgson (2006) adds the enabling dimension: "institutions both constrain and enable behavior" — the rules of language don't just limit what we can say, they make communication possible.

4.2 — Old Institutionalism, Behavioralism, and the New Institutionalism

Old institutionalism (≈1900–1950) focused on formal government structures and constitutional arrangements — descriptive, largely a-theoretical. Behavioralism (1950s–1960s) swung to the opposite extreme: only observable individual behavior matters; institutions are just arenas in which actors play out their pre-existing preferences. New institutionalism (from the 1980s) restored institutions to the center, but treated them as "dynamic political configurations that affect the mobilization of interests" (Immergut 2021) — not just legal containers, but active shapers of preferences and outcomes. Shepsle's key insight: the stability of collective decisions in legislatures is explained by institutional structure, not just individual preferences — "structure-induced equilibrium."

4.3 — Three Variants of New Institutionalism

Rational choice institutionalism sees institutions as voluntary agreements that solve collective action problems — they emerge because actors benefit from having predictable rules. Change occurs when preferences shift or external shocks make old arrangements inefficient. Historical institutionalism emphasizes that institutions are inherited from the past — they generate path dependency (current choices are constrained by past choices) and change through critical junctures (moments of discontinuity when new paths become possible). Sociological institutionalism focuses on how institutions embody cultural norms, cognitive frames, and taken-for-granted assumptions — actors follow rules not because it maximizes utility but because it is "what is done here."

The three variants are not mutually exclusive — the debate has generated productive combinations. Hall & Taylor (1996) note: any complete account of institutions needs elements of all three.

4.4 — Electoral Systems as a Case Study

Electoral rules are formal institutions that translate votes into seats. The same underlying social cleavages (class, religion, region) produce very different party systems depending on electoral rules. Winner-takes-all single-member districts (UK, US) create strong incentives for strategic voting and two-party systems. Proportional representation (Netherlands, Sweden) lowers barriers to entry and produces fragmented, multi-party systems. Same interests — completely different institutional outcomes. This is Shepsle's "structure-induced equilibrium" made visible: the rules of the game shape who can play and how.

04 Theoretical Debates
Rational Choice vs. Historical vs. Sociological Institutionalism
Rational Choice (Shepsle)
Institutions = voluntary cooperative arrangements that solve collective action problems. Actors rationally design institutions to reduce transaction costs and generate predictable outcomes. Change occurs when preferences shift or when exogenous shocks make old arrangements suboptimal.
Historical (Thelen, Hacker)
Institutions are inherited — current choices are constrained by past ones (path dependency). Change is slow and rare, occurring through critical junctures that open new paths. Timing and sequencing matter: the order in which institutions develop shapes later outcomes.
Sociological variant (Dobbin): Institutions embody cultural norms and cognitive frames. Actors follow rules not because it maximizes utility (rational choice) but because it is taken-for-granted, "what we do here." Change occurs through ideational and discursive shifts.
What this reveals → Each approach captures a real dimension. Rational choice explains institutional design; historical institutionalism explains persistence and path dependency; sociological explains why institutions are seen as legitimate rather than just convenient. The most powerful analyses combine all three.
Old Institutionalism vs. Behavioralism vs. New Institutionalism
Old Institutionalism (≈1900–1950)
Focus on formal constitutional and legal structures. Descriptive, comparative, largely a-theoretical. Institutions as the main object — but studied as fixed legal facts, not dynamic political configurations.
Behavioralism (1950s–60s) → New Institutionalism (1980s+)
Behavioralism: only observable individual behavior matters; institutions are arenas, not causes. New institutionalism: institutions independently shape outcomes — the "structure-induced equilibrium" proves that preferences alone cannot explain legislative stability.
What this reveals → The field's intellectual history shows a pendulum: from institutions (old), to actors (behavioralism), back to institutions (new) — but with a crucial upgrade: new institutionalism treats institutions as dynamic and politically produced, not just as legal facts.

01 Executive TL;DR

⚡ 60-second pitch

Political competition borrows from economics but differs fundamentally: policies are mutually exclusive, voters don't always get what they vote for, and aggregating preferences is mathematically treacherous (Condorcet, Arrow). Downs (1957) shows rational candidates converge to the median voter — but this requires symmetric distributions, complete information, and a single policy dimension. Strøm (1992) draws out the model's implications: representative efficiency, non-exploitation of leaders, policy continuity. Schumpeter (1962) is more cynical: the electorate is too irrational for Downsian competition; democracy is just elite competition for votes, nothing more. In practice, competition is structured by institutions — committees, party rules, procedural norms — as much as by voter preferences.

5.1 — Political vs. Economic Competition

The class begins with an analogy and then systematically breaks it down. Economic competition theory (Stigler 1972) predicts that more competitors → lower prices, more efficiency. Political science borrows this logic — more parties = more competition = more representation. But the analogy breaks down in important ways. Policies are mutually exclusive: unlike markets where consumers can buy from multiple firms, only one policy can be implemented at a time. Voters don't receive what they purchase: voting for a party does not guarantee that party's policies will be implemented. More parties ≠ more competition: as parties multiply, coalition bargaining can push outcomes toward the status quo. Aggregation is mathematically hard: Condorcet's paradox shows that majority preferences can cycle even when all individual preferences are rational.

5.2 — The Downsian Model and the Median Voter

Anthony Downs (1957), building on Hotelling's spatial model of economic competition, shows that in a two-party system with a normally distributed electorate on a single left-right dimension, both parties will rationally converge to the position of the median voter. The candidates' sole motivation is winning office (not implementing policy) — policy is an "instrument to be used opportunistically" (Roemer 2001). The implications: electoral competition produces moderate governments, limits party leaders' ability to pursue private interests, and generates policy continuity (only changes when voter preferences shift).

The model has been under sustained empirical challenge. The assumption of a single policy dimension fails in multi-issue politics. The assumption of a symmetric distribution fails in polarized electorates. The 2024 US election, for instance, showed both parties moving away from the median rather than toward it — a direct anomaly for Downsian theory.

5.3 — Schumpeter's Competitive Elitism

Schumpeter (1962) offers a radically different model. He defines democracy not by its outputs or its deliberative quality, but minimally: democracy is an "institutional arrangement for arriving at political decisions in which individuals acquire the power to decide by means of a competitive struggle." The electorate is not a rational principal choosing among policy platforms — it is a mass incapable of informed judgment, susceptible to manipulation, a "stampede." Democracy is justified not because it produces good policy, but because it is a peaceful mechanism for selecting and removing elites.

Schumpeter's pessimism anticipates Arrow's impossibility theorem (1951): there is no mechanism that can aggregate individual preferences into a consistent collective preference — any voting rule violates at least one reasonable condition. This is generalized by Condorcet's paradox: three voters with perfectly rational individual preferences can produce collectively irrational cycles. The implication: "the general will" or "the will of the people" is mathematically incoherent.

5.4 — Three Principles of Aggregation + Structured Competition

The class identifies three alternative logics for aggregating preferences: majority rule (the option with the most votes wins — simple but vulnerable to Condorcet cycles), pluralism (seek compromise among organized interest groups — the US model with separation of powers and federalism), and collective rationality (entrust unelected experts with decisions on goals that command broad consensus — independent central banks, regulatory agencies). Each solves a different problem and creates different ones.

Finally, competition in practice is structured by institutions. In Congress, the majority party controls the floor agenda, committee assignments, and scheduling — competition is "structure-induced" (Shepsle). The same is true in non-democratic systems: in China, elite competition within the CCP is real and consequential, but it occurs within institutional constraints that prevent it from threatening the Party's monopoly.

Downs vs. Schumpeter — What is Electoral Competition?
Downs (1957) — Median Voter
Rational candidates maximize votes by converging to the median voter. Policy is an instrument, not a goal. Result: moderate, centrist governments. Competition is efficient — it minimizes aggregate policy distance between government and voters.

Majority Rule
The option with the most votes wins. Simple, direct. But vulnerable to Condorcet cycles and majority tyranny (can systematically exclude minorities).

Methods & Causal Inference

Aizaz Hussain
01 Executive TL;DR
⚡ 60-second pitch

The seminar covers three methodological foundations. Causality vs. correlation: two variables moving together does not mean one causes the other — a lurking third variable (Z) may drive both. The gold standard is the RCT, but in political science we usually rely on quasi-experiments instead. Inductive vs. deductive: deductive = start from a theory and test it (top-down); inductive = start from observations and build a theory (bottom-up). Positivism vs. constructivism: positivism assumes an objective, discoverable reality; constructivism argues reality is socially constructed and meaning-dependent. These are not just philosophical debates — they determine what counts as valid evidence in a dissertation.

Causality vs. Correlation

Correlation: a statistical relationship where two variables move together — if X increases, Y also increases (or decreases). Does not imply direction. Example from seminar: ice cream sales and drowning incidents are highly correlated — but eating ice cream does not cause drowning. Both are caused by a third variable: hot weather.

Causality: a directional relationship where a change in X produces a change in Y. Requires ruling out the lurking (confounding) variable. The ideal: the counterfactual — what would have happened to the same subject if the treatment had not occurred? Since we cannot observe the same subject in two states simultaneously, we approximate with experiments.

RCT (Randomized Controlled Trial): randomly assign subjects to treatment or control group. Random assignment ensures the only systematic difference between groups is the treatment itself — "breaks" the influence of confounding variables. Gold standard. But often impossible in political science (cannot randomly assign minimum wages to countries).

Quasi-experiments: when randomization is impossible, mimic it using real-world variation. Four main methods: Difference-in-Differences (compare change in affected vs. control group over time), Regression Discontinuity (compare just above/below a cutoff), Instrumental Variables (find Z that affects X but not Y directly), Matching (pair treated/untreated units that are identical on all other variables).

Inductive vs. Deductive Reasoning

Deductive (theory-testing, top-down): start with a general theory → derive a testable hypothesis → collect data → confirm or reject. Logic: if the premises are true, the conclusion must be true. Example: "If the Democratic Peace Theory is true, then two democracies should not go to war." Commonly used in quantitative, large-N research.

Inductive (theory-building, bottom-up): start with specific observations → identify a pattern → formulate a tentative hypothesis → develop a theory. Conclusions are probabilistic. Example: "I notice rural voters shifted toward populist candidates in three elections — perhaps economic displacement leads to populist support." Commonly used in qualitative case studies and fieldwork.

Key difference: deductive tests existing theories; inductive generates new ones. Neither is superior — the choice depends on the research question.

Positivism vs. Constructivism

Positivism: there is an objective reality that exists independently of our perceptions. Political phenomena can be measured and studied like natural science. Key assumptions: value-neutrality (researcher can be objective), regularity (social world follows discoverable laws), goal of causal explanation and prediction. Methodology: predominantly quantitative.

Constructivism: reality is socially constructed. Facts do not "speak for themselves" — they are given meaning through human language, culture, and shared ideas. Key assumptions: intersubjectivity (concepts like "sovereignty" exist only because we agree they do), context matters (cannot understand action without social context and actor's intent), goal of interpretation and understanding "how" not just "why." Methodology: predominantly qualitative, discourse analysis, ethnography.

03 Theoretical Debates
Positivism vs. Constructivism — Two Worldviews
Positivism
Reality is objective and external. Researcher = objective observer. Goal: generalizable causal laws (X → Y). Methodology: quantitative, large datasets, formal modeling. Example question: "Do democracies fight fewer wars?"
Constructivism
Reality is subjective and socially created. Researcher = part of the social world. Goal: context-specific meanings and interpretations. Methodology: qualitative, discourse analysis, ethnography. Example question: "How did 'democracy' become a shared international norm?"
What this reveals → Neither is simply "right." They answer different questions. Positivism is better for causal effects (does X cause Y?). Constructivism is better for meaning and process (how does X come to mean what it means?). A dissertation can draw on both — but must be explicit about which epistemology it is using and why.
Deductive vs. Inductive — Two Logics of Inquiry
Deductive (Top-Down)
Theory first, data second. Tests existing theories. Conclusion is certain if premises are true. Common in quantitative/large-N research. Risk: theory may not fit the case; confirmation bias.
Inductive (Bottom-Up)
Data first, theory second. Generates new theories. Conclusion is probabilistic. Common in qualitative/case study research. Risk: overgeneralization from limited cases; pattern-matching without mechanism.
What this reveals → Most good research is actually abductive — moving back and forth between theory and data. A researcher may start inductively (noticing a pattern), generate a hypothesis, then test it deductively. The seminar's point: know which direction you are going, and why.

Worked example — prompt "Compounding/Z factor" (potential exam question)

Your question: "What is the compounding/Z factor and why does it matter for causal inference in political science?"

Define: The Z factor (confounding/lurking variable) is a third variable that causes both X and Y, creating a spurious correlation between them. Seminar example: hot weather (Z) causes both ice cream sales (X) and drowning incidents (Y). The correlation between X and Y is real but the causal relationship is not.

Argue: In political science, confounders are ubiquitous. Richer countries tend to have both better institutions AND higher growth — but which causes which? Both may be caused by historical factors (colonial legacy, geography). Without controlling for Z, any regression of institutions on growth is biased.

Plot twist: This is exactly why AJR (2001) used settler mortality as an instrument — it affects institutions (X) but has no direct path to economic growth (Y) except through institutions. The instrument isolates the causal effect of X on Y by finding variation in X that is uncontaminated by Z.

Beyond class conclusion: The Z problem is not just technical — it is epistemological. Constructivists argue that in social science, the "lurking variable" problem is irreducible: context always matters, and no statistical control can fully capture the meaning-laden character of political phenomena.

03 Potential Questions
7 potential questions — directly from seminar slides (Hussain)

These are not guaranteed exam questions — they are examples of how to turn a vague prompt into an essay question. Practice turning each into a thesis.

Q1 — "Can we classify political regimes?"
💡
Key concepts to define: political regime, democracy (Lipset 1995), authoritarianism, typology.
Thesis direction: Classification is possible but contested — positivist typologies (Polity scores, Freedom House) capture observable features, but constructivists argue regime labels are themselves political acts that reflect the classifier's worldview.
Beyond class: Hungary, India — regimes that pass electoral tests but fail liberal ones. "Competitive authoritarianism" challenges binary regime classifications.
Q2 — "The higher the competition, the better the democracy?"
💡
Key concepts: competition, democracy (Lipset 1995), median voter (Downs/Roemer), Schumpeter (1962), Condorcet paradox, Arrow (1951).
Thesis direction (plot twist): Competition is necessary but not sufficient. More competition can produce polarization (violates Downsian assumptions), aggregation failures (Condorcet), and elite capture (Schumpeter). Quality of competition depends on institutional design, not quantity.
Beyond class: US polarization 2010–2024 as empirical counter-case to Downsian convergence.
Q3 — "What is the compounding/Z factor?"
💡
Key concepts: correlation vs. causality, confounding variable, counterfactual, RCT, quasi-experiments, instrumental variable.
Thesis direction: The Z factor is the fundamental obstacle to causal inference in social science — and solving it requires quasi-experimental designs that exploit exogenous variation. But constructivists argue the Z problem is irreducible in the social world: context always confounds.
Link to course: AJR settler mortality = instrumental variable solving Z problem. Card & Krueger DiD = quasi-experiment avoiding Z.
Q4 — "What is the difference between Schumpeter's and Dahl's understanding of competition?"
💡
Key concepts: Schumpeter (1962) — democratic method, electoral mass as stampede. Dahl (1957, 1961) — pluralism, power dispersed, Who Governs?
Thesis direction: Schumpeter sees competition as elite circulation; Dahl sees it as preference aggregation. The difference is epistemological: Schumpeter is skeptical of voter rationality; Dahl is empirical about actual decision outcomes. Both miss the institutional dimension (Shepsle, Cox & McCubbins).
Structure 3 plan: Schumpeter's competitive elitism → Dahl's pluralist challenge → institutional structures as the missing dimension.
Q5 — "Does the electoral system affect voting decisions?"
💡
Key concepts: electoral institutions (Class 4), structure-induced equilibrium (Shepsle), strategic voting, party systems (UK vs Netherlands case).
Thesis direction: Yes — but through two mechanisms: direct (rules change what choices are available — strategic voting under FPTP) and indirect (rules shape which parties exist at all, which shapes the choice set). Institutions → party system → voter choice → not just "institutions → voter choice."
Beyond class: Ranked-choice voting debate in the US — institutional reform as electoral reform.
Q6 — "Does the use of economic methods make sense in political science?"
💡
Key concepts: positivism vs. constructivism, economic analogies in poli-sci (Downs, Stigler, Weingast), limits of rational choice.
Thesis direction (plot twist): Economic methods are powerful for modeling strategic behavior and testing causal claims — but political competition differs fundamentally from economic competition (policies are mutually exclusive, voters don't always get what they purchase, aggregation fails). The transplant is productive but requires careful adaptation.
Authors to mobilize: Stigler (1972) on the analogy, Schumpeter on its limits, Condorcet/Arrow on why aggregation fails, quasi-experiments as the methodological bridge.
Q7 — "Explain institutions and draw a comparative analysis of state/non-state institutions"
💡
Key concepts: North (1990), Moe (2015), Hodgson (2006), three new institutionalisms (Hall & Taylor 1996), formal vs. informal.
Thesis direction: Institutions are not limited to the state — chieftaincy in Malawi, the Church, corporate governance are all institutions in North's sense. The distinction state/non-state is empirically useful but analytically less important than the distinction formal/informal and enabling/constraining.
Structure 3 plan: Define institutions + state-centered view → non-state institutions (Malawi case, Germany vocational training) → what this reveals: institutions precede and transcend the state; the state is itself an institution.
05 Cross-Class Synthesis
Why cross-class synthesis gets you to 16.5–20

The marking scheme's top band requires "broader social and political implications" and going "beyond class material." The strongest way to do this is to connect concepts across classes — showing that power, ideas, institutions, and competition are not separate topics but different lenses on the same political reality. Five synthetic angles below.

Synthesis 1 — How do dominant ideas become institutionalized?
Class 3 (Ideas) + Class 4 (Institutions) + Class 2 (Power)
Ideas alone do not change policy — they must become embedded in institutions to have lasting effect. And embedding ideas in institutions requires power.
Hall (1993): the monetarist paradigm shift required not just new ideas but their institutionalization in independent central banks (North 1990). But why did monetarism win? Because it served the interests of financial actors who had the power (Carpenter's directive + gatekeeping) to push it onto the agenda. The full explanation requires all three classes.
Contemporary: The institutionalization of "tech solutionism" (AI as governance tool) — a new idea (Class 3) becoming embedded in regulatory frameworks (Class 4), promoted by actors with gatekeeping power over the policy agenda (Class 2).
Synthesis 2 — Can competition function without the right institutions?
Class 5 (Competition) + Class 4 (Institutions) + Class 1 (Science of Politics)
Downsian competition (Class 5) assumes a context it cannot explain: the institutional framework that makes competition possible (Class 4). The question "does competition produce good democracy?" is unanswerable without specifying the institutional rules.
Shepsle (1983) shows outcomes are structure-induced — the same competition under different rules produces different results. Class 1's positive/normative distinction applies: "competition is good for democracy" is a normative claim that requires a positive assessment of the institutional conditions under which it holds.
Contemporary: Platform competition (Twitter/X, TikTok) — competition exists, but the institutional rules (who can moderate, who owns the infrastructure) determine whether it produces deliberation or polarization.
Synthesis 3 — Is power over ideas the deepest form of power?
Class 2 (Power) + Class 3 (Ideas) + Class 4 (Institutions)
Carpenter's conceptual power (Class 2) and Hall's policy paradigm (Class 3) converge on the same insight: the most effective power is the power to define what is thinkable. Institutions (Class 4) are the mechanism through which this power is stabilized and reproduced.
Foucault (1980): power produces knowledge. Hall (1993): paradigms define what counts as a problem. North (1990): informal institutions (norms, beliefs) are as binding as formal rules. Together: dominant ideas + institutional embedding = the most durable form of power — invisible because it shapes the very categories used to evaluate it.
Contemporary: The "there is no alternative" (TINA) logic of neoliberalism — an ideational paradigm (Hall) that became institutionalized (North) and used gatekeeping power (Schattschneider) to organize alternative ideas out of the policy agenda.
Synthesis 4 — Why do political scientists disagree? Methods as ideology
Class 1 (Science) + Class 2 (Power) + Class 3 (Ideas) + Class 6 (Methods)
The positivism/constructivism debate (Class 1 and 6) is itself a power struggle over what counts as legitimate knowledge in political science — and the dominant paradigm at any moment reflects institutional power in the discipline, not epistemic superiority.
Foucault's power/knowledge (Class 2): "the exercise of power perpetually creates knowledge." Bourdieu (1989): classification struggles apply to academic disciplines too — who gets to define "good science" is itself political. Hall's paradigm concept (Class 3) applies to political science itself: the behavioral revolution and the quantitative turn were paradigm shifts, not just methodological improvements.
Essay angle: "Does the use of economic methods make sense in political science?" (Q6 from potential questions) — now answered not just with Downs vs. Schumpeter, but with the epistemological critique that the dominance of economic methods reflects power in the discipline, not just methodological superiority.
Synthesis 5 — Can non-state institutions be more powerful than the state?
Class 4 (Institutions) + Class 2 (Power) + Class 3 (Ideas)
North's definition of institutions (Class 4) encompasses non-state actors. When non-state institutions (corporations, platforms, churches, professional associations) exercise directive, gatekeeping, and conceptual power (Class 2), they may constrain political outcomes more powerfully than formal state institutions.
Gramsci (1971): the Church maintained power through hegemony — making its worldview appear as common sense (conceptual power). Pinto (2017): tobacco firms reshaped the scientific agenda through ignorance production (gatekeeping power). Moe (2015): institutional design reflects power — non-state actors design institutions to protect their interests. Hall & Taylor (1996): sociological institutionalism shows how non-state norms and frames can be as binding as laws.
Direct exam link: Q7 "Explain institutions and draw a comparative analysis of state/non-state institutions" — this synthesis gives you a 17+ answer by connecting institutional analysis to power theory.
04 Worked Practice
Practice exercise — turning a vague prompt into an essay

The examiner writes: "Power." Follow these four steps before writing a single sentence of the essay:

Step 1 — Identify the concept(s): Power. Which dimension? Directive / gatekeeping / conceptual. Weber's domination. Foucault's productive power.

Step 2 — Identify the tension: Is power always observable? Can democratic systems fully account for it? Does power require an agent?

Step 3 — Form a question: "Is power always visible? A critical assessment of its three faces and their democratic implications."

Step 4 — Draft your thesis (one sentence): "In this essay, I argue that power operates at three increasingly invisible levels — directive, gatekeeping, and conceptual — and that democratic theory has systematically underestimated the two deeper faces at the cost of effective accountability."

Now write: hook (concrete case — Dahl vs Mills) → problematize (same country, opposite conclusions) → thesis (above) → road map (I will first… then… before concluding that…). You have a strong introduction. The essay almost writes itself.

13/20 version of a conclusion: "In conclusion, power has three faces. Directive power is about behavior. Gatekeeping power is about agenda control. Conceptual power is about cognition. As Foucault shows, power is productive. Therefore we need to analyze all three faces to understand political life."

What is missing: No original claim. No broader implication. No thought-provoking argument. Just a summary.

17/20 upgrade: "In conclusion, the three-face framework (Carpenter 2010) reveals a democratic paradox: the deeper the face of power, the less democratic accountability can reach it. Directive power can be contested through elections; gatekeeping power can be challenged through agenda-setting movements; but conceptual power — the power to define what is thinkable — may be structurally immune to democratic challenge. In the digital age, algorithmic curation extends this deepest face of power to unprecedented scale: not a single actor imposing a worldview, but distributed systems shaping what millions believe is 'normal' without any identifiable command chain. This is Foucault's (1980) insight that power 'is proportional to its ability to hide its own mechanisms' — now instantiated at a civilizational level. Future research must ask whether democratic accountability mechanisms, designed for the first face of power, can be redesigned for the third."
